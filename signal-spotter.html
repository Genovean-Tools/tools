Signal Spotter

You're not diagnosing anything. You're simply being honest with yourself about what you've already noticed — before you talk yourself out of it again.

How to use this tool. Work through each signal below. If you've seen it — even once — open it, mark how often, and add a note. At the end you'll have a clear picture of what's actually been happening. There are no right or wrong answers here.

Daily life & routines
1 Missing or forgetting bills and payments Not marked

Late notices arriving, unopened mail piling up, utilities being threatened or cut off. These are often the first financial signals families notice — and they're easy to explain away as a busy week. The question is whether it's becoming a pattern.

If you're not sure what to look for

Look for unopened envelopes. Ask casually about a bill or subscription. Check whether they mention anything being "sorted out." You don't need to investigate — just observe and note what you see.

2 Medication not taken consistently Not marked

Skipped doses, mixed-up pills, or confusion about what they're taking and when. This is one of the more serious early signals because it directly affects health — but parents often cover for it, so it can be hard to see.

If you're not sure what to look for

Notice if pill bottles are in unexpected places, if they mention side effects that seem off, or if they're vague when you ask how they're managing. You're not checking up on them — you're paying attention.

3 Meals being skipped or diet becoming very limited Not marked

Cooking becoming too complicated, loss of appetite, the same few foods on repeat, or evidence that the fridge and pantry are going unstocked. Nutrition often quietly declines before anything else becomes visible.

If you're not sure what to look for

When you visit, take a casual look in the fridge. Notice whether they seem lighter or less energetic. Ask what they had for dinner yesterday — not as a check, just in conversation. Their answer often tells you more than you expect.

4 Home becoming harder to keep up Not marked

Dishes not getting done, laundry piling up, rooms that were always tidy now cluttered, maintenance being ignored. You're not looking for perfection — you're looking for a clear change from their normal.

How to notice without making it about cleanliness

Think about what their home usually looked like six months ago. If someone who always kept a clean kitchen now has dishes sitting for days, that shift matters far more than the dishes themselves.

Memory & thinking
5 Repeating the same question or story in the same conversation Not marked

Everyone forgets and repeats occasionally. The signal here is when it happens within the same conversation, or when the same story comes out every single time you visit. That shift from occasional to pattern is what matters.

What counts as a signal

One repetition in a long conversation isn't the signal. The signal is a pattern — the same question, the same story, within minutes or across every visit. Start noting how often it happens and when.

6 Confusion about dates, the day of the week, or recent events Not marked

Getting the day wrong, not remembering an appointment or visit that happened recently, or being genuinely surprised when told something they should know. The key is whether it's new, and whether it's happening more frequently.

How to tell this apart from normal forgetfulness

Most people mix up days occasionally. The shift to notice is when confusion becomes more frequent, when they seem genuinely disoriented rather than briefly forgetful, or when they're surprised to learn something recent they participated in.

7 Difficulty following conversations or making decisions Not marked

Losing the thread of a conversation, seeming confused in group settings that used to be easy, or becoming unusually indecisive about things they would previously have handled without a second thought.

What this looks like in everyday moments

Watch for them asking you to repeat yourself more than usual, trailing off mid-sentence, or seeming overwhelmed by choices that wouldn't have been difficult before. It often shows up first in noisy or busy environments.

8 Getting lost or confused in familiar places Not marked

Difficulty navigating a neighbourhood they've lived in for decades, getting turned around driving a route they've taken hundreds of times, or seeming uncertain about how to get somewhere familiar.

Why this signal is easy to miss

Parents often cover for this by avoiding driving or outings altogether. If you notice they're going out less, ask gently whether they've been getting around okay. Reduced activity can be the signal behind the signal.

Physical health & safety
9 Changes in how they move, walk, or keep their balance Not marked

Slower walking, holding onto furniture more, a shuffling gait, hesitation going up or down stairs. Falls are one of the most serious risks for aging adults — and the signs usually show up well before a fall actually happens.

How to observe without making them self-conscious

Watch how they get up from a chair, how they move through the kitchen, whether they reach for support in ways they didn't used to. You don't need to comment — just notice and log what you see.

10 Unexplained bruises, cuts, or signs of a fall Not marked

Visible marks they don't explain clearly, or a vague story about how it happened. Many falls go unreported — parents don't want to worry you or risk losing their independence. The injury you can see may not be the only one that's happened.

How to raise this without causing defensiveness

Don't make it an interrogation. Try: "I noticed that bruise — did something happen?" Keep your tone curious, not alarmed. If they deflect, note it down and watch for more signs rather than pushing in the moment.

11 Noticeable weight loss or physical appearance changes Not marked

Clothes that used to fit now hanging loose, a face that looks thinner than you remember, or a general physical decline you notice after not seeing them for a few months. Gradual changes can be hard to see when you're visiting regularly.

If you only see them often and can't tell

Ask someone who sees them less frequently what they've noticed. Look at photos from a year ago compared to now. Sometimes distance — in time or in person — makes the change easier to see clearly.

12 Driving becoming concerning — near misses, new dents, avoiding driving Not marked

New scrapes on the car, stories about close calls, refusing to drive at night or on highways, or simply driving much less than they used to. Independence is tied to driving for many parents — this is a signal that often comes with a lot of emotion attached.

How to approach this carefully

Note what you observe without raising it immediately. Look at the car when you visit. If you're in the car with them, pay attention without commenting. Driving conversations need to be timed carefully — this tool is about noticing, not yet about having that talk.

Emotional & social signals
13 Withdrawing from people or activities they used to enjoy Not marked

Stopping something they loved — a club, a hobby, regular visits with friends — without a clear reason. Withdrawal can signal depression, embarrassment about something changing, or simply that managing social life is taking more energy than it used to.

How to spot what's changed

Think about what they used to do six months ago that they're not doing now. Ask about it naturally: "Are you still going to [thing]?" Listen to how they explain it, not just what they say. Deflection or a vague answer is itself a signal.

14 Mood or personality changes — irritability, sadness, or anxiety Not marked

A parent who was generally even-tempered becoming short-tempered or easily frustrated. Increased anxiety about things that didn't used to worry them. A persistent low mood that isn't connected to a specific event. These shifts can be early signs of cognitive change, depression, or simply the emotional weight of their own awareness that something is different.

How to hold this without reacting to it

When a parent becomes irritable or anxious, it's tempting to respond to the mood rather than look behind it. Note what triggers the shift if you can. The emotion is often less important than the pattern underneath it.

Financial warning signs
15 Signs of scam vulnerability or unusual purchases Not marked

Mention of a lottery they've won, a charity they've started giving to that you don't recognise, unusual purchases arriving in the mail, or heightened secrecy about money matters. Older adults are disproportionately targeted by financial scams, and cognitive changes make this risk higher.

How to raise this gently

Frame it as general concern, not an accusation. "I've been reading a lot about phone scams targeting people lately — has anything like that come your way?" opens a door without putting them on the defensive.

16 Vagueness or avoidance when financial matters come up Not marked

Changing the subject when money comes up, being unusually secretive about accounts or documents they were always open about, or being unable to explain their financial situation in the way they once could. This doesn't always mean something is wrong — but it's worth noting.

Why this signal matters more than it seems

Financial capacity is often one of the first things to become vulnerable, but one of the last things families talk about. If you can see a pattern of avoidance forming, note it now. That information becomes important when you reach the planning phase.
